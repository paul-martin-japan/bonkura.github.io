There were heavy snows in the February of 2014. The roads roads became temporarily impassable; neither was it easy to move around on foot. The snow made our lives miserable here in Fukushima for several days. And so it was that one sunny morning in March, when the snow had gone, I surprised myself as I found myself saying "Diffugere nives" without thinking and with a kind of glee.

    Sometime earlier I had come across an anecdote about A. E. Housman, who is said to have ended a lecture on Horace in May 1914 by asking his students to consider the ode in question (Book 4, Ode VII)  "simply as poetry," and to make his point is said to have read them his own translation. Uncharacteristically, he is also described as having pointed out that this ode of Horace is "the most beautiful poem in ancient literature." The anecdote prompted me to look up the ode in question, which did not strike me as any more impressive than some of the other odes. It was not until I read Housman's translation of Ode VII that the scales fell from my eyes. The original and the translation, both tours de force, are extraordinary. It took the poet and teacher Housman to illuminate the craftsmanship of the Latin with his own.

Diffugere nives Horace (Odes, Book 4, Ode VII)

The snows are fled A. E. Housman (More Poems)
